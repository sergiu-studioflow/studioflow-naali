Introduction
This document serves as the foundational intelligence layer for all AI creative systems built for Naali. It is not a summary of data. It is a strategic operating system designed to ensure every piece of creative output - whether generated by AI or reviewed by AI - is psychologically resonant, legally compliant, and commercially effective.
The intelligence contained here was synthesised from 21,714 customer survey responses, six months of ad performance data, French regulatory compliance documentation, and direct input from Naali's founding team. Every insight has been pressure-tested against what actually converts in paid media.

Part One: The Naali Customer
Who She Is
The Naali customer is not defined by demographics. She is defined by a moment in her life.
She is a woman - typically between 30 and 55 - who has reached a breaking point she didn't see coming. She has spent years managing everything: her career, her household, her children, her relationships, her ageing parents. She has been the person everyone relies on. And now, for the first time, she is failing at the one thing she always managed to hold together: herself.
She doesn't sleep properly anymore. She wakes up at 3am with her mind racing through tomorrow's obligations. She snaps at her children over small things and then feels crushing guilt. She has gained weight she cannot explain. Her hair is falling out in clumps. She cries in her car before walking into work. She has begun to wonder if this is just what life is now.
She is not looking for a supplement. She is looking for proof that she is not broken.
This is the person Naali serves. Understanding her is not optional - it is the entire foundation of effective creative.
The Language She Uses
When asked to describe how she feels in three words, the Naali customer does not use clinical language. She does not say "I have anxiety" or "I suffer from chronic stress." She speaks in the language of exhaustion and defeat:
"Fatiguée. Stressée. Vidée." (Exhausted. Stressed. Empty.)
"Épuisée. Anxieuse. Perdue." (Depleted. Anxious. Lost.)
"À bout. Irritable. Triste." (At breaking point. Irritable. Sad.)
The word "fatiguée" appears 368 times in the survey data - more than double any other descriptor. This is critical: the Naali customer does not primarily identify as "stressed" or "anxious." She identifies as exhausted. Stress is the cause. Exhaustion is the lived experience.
This distinction matters for creative. Headlines that lead with "stressed?" will underperform headlines that lead with "exhausted?" or "running on empty?" because the latter matches her internal language.
What She Is Afraid Of
Her fears are not abstract. They are specific and immediate:
Fear of permanent damage: "Que ça puisse impacter ma santé" - that this state is doing lasting harm to her body. She has noticed physical symptoms: her skin has aged, her hair is thinning, she has developed psoriasis, her digestion is destroyed. She fears she is watching herself deteriorate.
Fear of harming her children: "Mon mal-être qui va se reporter sur mon enfant" - that her emotional state is damaging her kids. She sees herself snapping at them. She sees them walking on eggshells around her. This is perhaps her deepest shame.
Fear of losing herself: "Ne plus être moi-même" - that the person she used to be is gone. She remembers being capable, optimistic, energetic. That woman feels like a stranger now.
Fear of it getting worse: "Que ça empire" - that this is not the bottom. That there is further to fall.
These fears are the emotional fuel of high-converting creative. They are not to be exploited crudely, but they must be acknowledged. The customer needs to feel seen before she will trust a solution.
What She Has Already Tried
The Naali customer is not new to seeking help. She has tried:
Meditation apps (she couldn't stick with them)
Herbal teas and natural remedies (too weak to notice)
Her doctor (who offered antidepressants she doesn't want)
Therapy (helpful but expensive and time-consuming)
Exercise (she knows she should but has no energy)
Magnesium supplements (some effect, but not enough)
Ignoring it and pushing through (her default, now failing)
This history of failed solutions creates both an obstacle and an opportunity. The obstacle: she is sceptical. She has been disappointed before. The opportunity: she is still searching. She has not given up.
Effective creative acknowledges this scepticism directly. "You've tried everything" is a hook that works because it validates her experience before asking her to try again.
Why She Buys Naali
Analysis of 14,512 purchase responses reveals the primary conversion drivers:
1. Social proof at scale The single most common conversion factor is reviews. Not influencer endorsements - customer reviews. She reads them looking for people like her. When she finds a review from a mother who describes the same symptoms, the same failed solutions, and then reports improvement, she converts.
2. The M6 television appearance Naali's appearance on "Qui veut être mon associé" (the French equivalent of Shark Tank) is mentioned repeatedly as a trust signal. It legitimises the brand. It transforms Naali from "random Instagram ad" to "company that was vetted on national television."
3. Natural positioning She does not want pharmaceuticals. She has likely been offered antidepressants or anti-anxiety medication and refused. The word "naturel" appears consistently in purchase reasons. Saffron as an ingredient carries credibility - it feels ancient, proven, safe.
4. The founders' story When she encounters content about why Naali was created - the personal story behind the brand - it builds trust. She is not buying from a faceless corporation. She is buying from people who understood the problem because they lived it.
5. Promotional offers Price is the dominant purchase barrier (mentioned 1,441 times). Discounts and bundle offers reduce the risk of trying something new. First-purchase promotions are essential to conversion.
Why She Reorders
The repeat purchase survey (4,947 responses) reveals what happens after she tries Naali:
She sleeps better. This is the most frequently reported benefit. Not "cured insomnia" - but noticeably improved sleep. She falls asleep faster. She wakes up less. She feels more rested.
She is calmer with her family. Multiple responses describe reduced irritability, fewer outbursts, more patience with children. This is the benefit she cares about most - not how she feels internally, but how she shows up for the people she loves.
It becomes a ritual. "C'est devenu un rituel" appears repeatedly. The act of taking the gummies each morning becomes a small act of self-care. It is a moment where she does something for herself.
She sees her children benefit. A significant segment of reorders comes from mothers who initially bought for themselves, saw results, and then started giving the product to their children - particularly children with ADHD, anxiety, or concentration issues. This is an underexploited market segment.

Part Two: The Naali Voice
Brand Positioning
Naali is not a wellness brand. Wellness brands sell aspiration - the yoga-body, the green-juice life, the calm minimalist aesthetic. That positioning alienates the Naali customer because she feels miles away from that image.
Naali is a relief brand. It sells the return to baseline. Not "become your best self" but "become yourself again." Not optimisation but restoration.
The visual and verbal language should reflect this:
Not aspirational imagery of serene women meditating at sunrise
Real imagery of women who look tired, overwhelmed, recognisable
Not promises of transformation
Acknowledgment of struggle followed by evidence of relief
Tone of Voice
Warm but not soft. The Naali voice understands that this customer does not have time for fluff. She is exhausted. She is sceptical. She needs directness wrapped in empathy.
Knowledgeable but not clinical. She wants to know the science works, but she does not want a lecture. Explain the mechanism simply. Do not use jargon unless it builds credibility (e.g., "saffron" carries weight; "adaptogenic compounds" does not).
Honest about limitations. Naali is not a miracle cure. It is a meaningful support. Creative that overpromises will attract customers who churn. Creative that sets realistic expectations attracts customers who stay.
Language Patterns That Convert
From analysis of high-performing creative and customer language:
Lead with exhaustion, not stress:
✓ "Épuisée sans savoir pourquoi?"
✓ "Vous vous réveillez fatiguée?"
✗ "Vous êtes stressée?"
Acknowledge failed attempts:
✓ "Vous avez tout essayé"
✓ "Les tisanes n'ont pas suffi"
✓ "Votre médecin propose des médicaments que vous ne voulez pas"
Focus on functional outcomes:
✓ "Retrouver un sommeil réparateur"
✓ "Être plus patiente avec vos enfants"
✓ "Arrêter de ruminer à 3h du matin"
✗ "Réduire votre stress" (too vague)
✗ "Améliorer votre bien-être" (too generic)
Use her vocabulary:
"Charge mentale" (mental load)
"À bout" (at breaking point)
"Craquer" (to crack/break down)
"Ruminer" (to ruminate/overthink)
"Lâcher prise" (to let go)
"Retrouver" (to find again/recover)

Part Three: Compliance Framework
The Regulatory Reality
Naali operates under EU Regulation (CE) n°1924/2006 governing health claims for food supplements. This regulation is strictly enforced in France by the DGCCRF. Non-compliance risks include:
Forced removal of advertising
Financial penalties
Reputational damage
Platform ad account restrictions
The creative systems built for Naali must treat compliance as non-negotiable. Every script, every static, every hook must pass through a compliance filter before production.

Prohibited Language
The following words and concepts are forbidden in all Naali creative:
Medical/Therapeutic Terms:
Symptômes (symptoms)
Cure / traitement (cure / treatment)
Dépression / antidépresseurs (depression / antidepressants)
Troubles anxieux (anxiety disorders)
TDAH (ADHD)
Stress chronique (chronic stress)
Maladie (disease/illness)
Guérir (to cure)
Soigner (to treat medically)
Mechanism Claims:
Cortisol (cannot claim to reduce/regulate)
Sérotonine (cannot claim to affect)
Neurotransmetteurs (neurotransmitters)
Hormone (cannot claim hormonal effects)
Strength Claims:
Sans effet secondaire (no side effects)
Sans dépendance (non-addictive)
Cliniquement prouvé (clinically proven) - discouraged
Scientifiquement prouvé (scientifically proven) - discouraged
Forbidden Verbs:
Stimuler (stimulate)
Activer (activate)
Renforcer (strengthen)
Améliorer (improve) - when applied to health conditions
Équilibrer (balance) - when applied to hormones/neurotransmitters
Réguler (regulate)
Corriger (correct)
Restaurer (restore)
Guérir (cure)

These verbs are compliant when used with authorised claims:
Participer à (participate in)
Jouer un rôle dans (play a role in)
Soutenir (support)
Aider à (help to)
Contribuer à (contribute to)
Maintenir (maintain)
Favoriser (promote/encourage)
Bon pour (good for)
Optimiser (optimise)
Compliant Copy Construction
The Linking Rule: Every general claim must be linked to a specific authorised claim via asterisk or footnote.
Non-Compliant:
"Nos gummies anti-stress vous aident à retrouver votre calme."
This is non-compliant because: (1) the claim is attributed to the product, not an ingredient; (2) no authorised claim is referenced.
Compliant:
"Retrouvez votre calme* grâce au safran et aux vitamines B."
Le safran aide à favoriser la détente. Les vitamines B3, B6, B9 et B12 contribuent à des fonctions psychologiques normales.
Non-Compliant:
"Le safran réduit le stress en régulant le cortisol."
This is non-compliant because: (1) "réduit" (reduces) is too strong; (2) cortisol mechanism claims are forbidden.
Compliant:
"Le cortisol est une hormone associée au stress. Le safran est utilisé pour favoriser la détente."
Note: The context is stated factually, then the ingredient claim is stated separately without linking them directly.
Testimonial Rules
Customer testimonials are permitted under specific conditions:
No health professionals: Cannot feature doctors, pharmacists, or medical professionals recommending the product
Health claims in testimonials = health claims: If a customer says "it cured my anxiety," that testimonial cannot be used
Satisfaction statistics are restricted: Claims like "90% of users report improved sleep" are non-compliant because they go beyond authorised claim language
Compliant testimonial approach:
General satisfaction: "Je suis satisfaite" ✓
Quality of sleep (tied to claim): "Je trouve un meilleur sommeil*" ✓
Emotional outcome without medical language: "Je me sens plus sereine" ✓
Non-compliant testimonials:
"Ça a soigné mon anxiété" ✗
"Mes symptômes de dépression ont disparu" ✗
"Je n'ai plus de crises d'angoisse" ✗
Clinical Study References
References to clinical studies are treated as non-specific health claims and must be accompanied by an authorised specific claim. Additionally:
Studies cannot be linked directly to the product
Studies cannot suggest effects beyond authorised claims
Percentage-based efficacy claims from studies are generally non-compliant

Part Four: Creative Intelligence
What Performs (Last 6 Months Analysis)
1. Human, Emotional, Lived Content
The highest-performing creative features real people sharing genuine experiences. Not polished testimonials - raw, imperfect, emotional delivery. The customer converts when she sees someone who looks and sounds like her describing symptoms she recognises.
Formats that work:
Genuine customer testimonials (unscripted feeling)
Daily-life stories showing the reality of stress/exhaustion
Couple or close-relationship interview formats
POV / first-person narratives
"Before and after" framed as internal relief, not external transformation
Why it works: Trust and identification outperform persuasion. The customer has been marketed to her entire life. She recognises sales tactics. Authenticity disarms her scepticism.
2. Social Proof at Scale
In the supplement category, scepticism is high. The customer assumes most products don't work. Overcoming this requires proof volume, not proof quality.
Formats that work:
Walls of reviews
Screenshots from social media comments
"Thousands of women like you" messaging
Business Suite / notification-style creatives
Warehouse / logistics footage (signals real business scale)
Why it works: One testimonial could be fake. A thousand reviews are harder to dismiss.
3. Founder-Led Education
Karim and the founding team explaining the product, the ingredients, and the science performs consistently. This is not about celebrity - it is about authority and transparency.
Formats that work:
Founder explaining why Naali was created
Founder walking through the science simply
"This is not magnesium... it's much better" style reframes
Podcast / interview-style content
Subscription explanation by founder
Why it works: The founder acts as a translator of complexity. The customer does not want to research saffron studies. She wants someone she trusts to have done it for her.
4. Science Made Digestible
The customer wants to know this is not snake oil. But she does not want a lecture. Scientific content performs when it answers doubt, not when it demonstrates intelligence.
Formats that work:
Simple ingredient + benefit callouts
Whiteboard / edutainment style videos
Stop-motion or illustrated explanations
"Clinically studied for 60/90 days" (especially for collagen)
Addressing scepticism directly: "You're right to be sceptical..."
Why it works: Science reassures without intimidating. The goal is credibility, not education.
5. Usage and Sensory Content
Showing the product being used reduces the friction of imagination. The customer can see exactly what she's getting, how she'll use it, and that it's easy.
Formats that work:
Close-ups of gummies, saffron threads, product texture
Morning routine integration footage
Children or adults taking the product happily
ASMR-style product content
"Easy routine" messaging
Why it works: Low effort is a key purchase driver. If it looks complicated, she won't try it.
6. Soft Agitation and Recognition
The customer needs to feel seen before she will consider a solution. Agitation content that mirrors her internal experience without dramatising it performs well.
Formats that work:
"Do these signs sound familiar?"
Lists of symptoms with visual checkmarks
Irritability / fatigue / overwhelm messaging
Burnout explanation content (educational, not alarmist)
Why it works: People engage when they feel understood, not when they feel diagnosed.
7. Native and "Ugly" Formats
Content that looks like organic social media outperforms content that looks like advertising.
Formats that work:
Phone notes app statics
Notification-style creatives
Post-it note aesthetics
Microsoft Paint / low-fi billboard style
"Secret discovery" UGC framing
Feed-blending visuals
Why it works: Low production signals honesty. High production signals budget spent on convincing her.
What Underperforms
1. Overly Polished Brand-Heavy Visuals Generic studio product shots, beauty-only statics without human context, high-gloss ads with no person in them. These look like every other supplement ad. They fail to stop the scroll.
2. Text-Heavy Without Emotional Angle Long ingredient lists with no framing. Educational content that explains what without explaining why she should care. Information does not equal motivation.
3. Promotion Without Narrative Discounts presented without context. "30% off" means nothing if she doesn't already want the product. Price-first messaging fails at top of funnel.
4. Abstract Benefits Vague wellness language. "Good for you." "Supports wellbeing." "Natural calm." These phrases are meaningless. The customer relates to specific moments, not abstract states.
5. Influencer Testimonials That Skip the Pain Point An influencer holding the product and saying "I love this" performs worse than a non-influencer describing her actual struggle. Relatability of the problem matters more than credibility of the endorser.

Part Five: Awareness Level Framework
Naali currently uses a "Poppy board" system to categorise scripts by awareness level. This framework must be embedded in all AI creative systems.
Level 1: Unaware
The customer does not know she has a problem that can be solved. She may not even have language for what she's experiencing.
Creative objective: Educate and intrigue. Make her recognise herself.
Hook style: Questions that provoke self-reflection. Educational content framed as discovery.
Examples:
"D'un point de vue neuroscientifique, qu'est-ce que le stress fait réellement dans le cerveau?"
"Pourquoi les personnes sous pression prolongée disent souvent: 'Je ne panique pas - je ne me sens juste plus calme'?"
Tone: Documentary. Curious. Scientific but accessible.
Warning: Pure unaware content can feel too academic. Balance education with emotional resonance.
Level 2: Problem Aware
The customer knows something is wrong. She feels the exhaustion, the irritability, the sleeplessness. She does not yet know there is a solution.
Creative objective: Validate her experience. Show her she is not alone. Name the problem.
Hook style: Symptom recognition. "This is what's happening to you."
Examples:
"Épuisée, irritable, l'esprit qui ne s'arrête jamais. Voilà ce que le stress fait à ton cerveau."
"Je ne ressentais plus rien. Ni joie, ni stress. Juste du vide. C'était un signal d'alarme."
Tone: Empathetic. First-person. Relatable.
This is Naali's strongest awareness level. Most of the customer base sits here.
Level 3: Solution Aware
The customer knows solutions exist. She has tried some. She may know about saffron or supplements generally. She does not yet know about Naali specifically.
Creative objective: Differentiate Naali. Explain why this solution is different from what she's tried.
Hook style: Comparison. "Why this works when others didn't."
Examples:
"Vous avez essayé le magnésium. Voici pourquoi ça n'a pas suffi."
"La différence entre le safran en gélule et le safran Naali."
Tone: Authoritative. Educational. Confident.
Level 4: Product Aware
The customer knows Naali exists. She has seen ads, visited the site, maybe added to cart. She has not purchased.
Creative objective: Overcome final objections. Build urgency. Provide proof.
Hook style: Objection handling. Social proof. Offer.
Examples:
"Vous hésitez encore? Voici ce que disent les 50,000 femmes qui ont essayé."
"La raison pour laquelle j'ai attendu 3 mois avant d'acheter - et pourquoi je regrette."
Tone: Direct. Proof-heavy. Action-oriented.
Level 5: Most Aware
The customer has purchased before. She knows the product works. She needs a reason to reorder now.
Creative objective: Remind. Reward loyalty. Introduce new products.
Hook style: Retention. Cross-sell. Subscription value.
Examples:
"Votre cure se termine bientôt - voici votre offre fidélité."
"Vous aimez l'Anti-Stress? Découvrez ce que le Magnésium peut ajouter."
Tone: Familiar. Appreciative. Exclusive.

Demographics: 38 years old, married, two children (6 and 9), works part-time in HR, lives in suburban Lyon.
Situation: Marie returned to work after her second maternity leave and never recovered her equilibrium. She manages the household logistics - school pickups, doctor appointments, meal planning, homework supervision - while also maintaining a professional role. Her husband helps but does not see the invisible labour. She is the project manager of a life that has too many tasks and not enough time.
Symptoms: She wakes up at 3am unable to stop her mind. She has gained 8kg in two years. She snaps at her children and then cries in the bathroom. She has started losing hair. Her libido is non-existent. She is exhausted but cannot sleep deeply.
What she's tried: Meditation app (gave up after 2 weeks), herbal sleep tea (didn't work), her GP (offered antidepressants, she refused), a weekend spa trip (temporary relief).

Objections to Naali: "Another supplement that won't work." "It's expensive." "I don't have time to research if it's legitimate."
What converts her: A testimonial from another mother describing the same symptoms. The M6 TV appearance as proof of legitimacy. A first-purchase discount to reduce risk.
Messaging that resonates: "Retrouver votre calme avec vos enfants." "Vous méritez une nuit complète." "Naturel, sans médicament."

Part Seven: Competitive Positioning
The Supplement Landscape
The French supplement market is crowded with stress and sleep products. Most competitors fall into predictable categories:
Pharmacy brands (Arkopharma, Pileje, etc.): Clinical positioning, medical aesthetic, sold through pharmacies. Credibility through distribution channel but lack emotional connection.
Wellness/Instagram brands: Aesthetic-first, influencer-driven, often lacking scientific rigour. Appeals to younger demographic seeking lifestyle products.
Amazon commodity products: Price-driven, low trust, interchangeable.
Naali's Differentiation
Naali occupies a distinct position: premium natural efficacy.
More scientific credibility than Instagram wellness brands
More emotional resonance than pharmacy brands
More trustworthy than Amazon commodities
Unique hero ingredient (saffron) vs. ubiquitous ingredients (magnesium, melatonin)
Messaging Implications
Do not compete on price. Naali is premium. Racing to the bottom destroys margin and attracts low-loyalty customers.
Do not compete on clinical coldness. Pharmacy brands own that territory. Naali wins on human connection.
Do compete on ingredient uniqueness. Saffron is differentiated. Most customers have never tried it. This is an advantage.
Do compete on proof volume. The volume of testimonials is a competitive moat. Few competitors can match 50,000+ customers and the reviews that come with that.
